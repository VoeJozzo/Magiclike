# Magic — works, no theory

## The resolution *(adopted 2026-06-09)*

The low/high-fantasy tension ([[tone]]) dissolved once the axis was named correctly: the variable isn't the *amount* of magic, it's the *understanding*. This world is high-fantasy in event-density and low-fantasy in epistemology. Magic happens constantly; nobody — peasant, magistrate, archmage — has a working theory of why.

> "The world is full of magic in the sense that *medieval England* was full of magic. (The fact that it's real in this world is irrelevant — in medieval England, *it was* real!)" — Joe

That's the whole posture in one image. A medieval villager lived among saints' relics, weather-curses, healing wells, and omens — a world saturated with magic, none of it systematized, all of it *real to the people in it*. Same here, except the relics actually work. The difference this makes to the inhabitants: **none.** Belief and craft, not mechanism, is how anyone relates to it.

> "'Gandalf is a wizard' and 'Gandalf can cast fireball if he prepares it during his long rest' are very different ways of conceptualizing magic." — Joe

Gandalf, not the D&D wizard. Magic is practiced as **craft** — technique inherited like smithing, rites that visibly function ([[blue]]: "the asking works even when the answering is ambiguous") with the mechanism as opaque to the practitioner as to the bystander. An archmage differs from a hedge-witch in *skill*, not in *comprehension*.

## The fourth register of the missing narrator

This slots into [[the-fall]] §narrator as the fourth leg:

- **Threads** — seen once, believed never.
- **Gods** — testimony everywhere, verdict nowhere.
- **History** — rungs, not dates.
- **Magic** — works, no theory.

And it makes the library's fall ([[blue]]) load-bearing: the library was the world's one serious attempt to *build* a theory of magic — the closest thing to a narrator the world ever tried to grow — and it burned. "Knowledge burns" stops being only a character note about blue and becomes part of why the whole world is the way it is.

## Rules-language ≠ world canon *(demarcation, parallel to [[art-language]])*

The game knows it runs on WUBRG mana, the stack, and the phase wheel. **The world doesn't.** Mechanics are the player's view, not the inhabitants'. No in-world character talks like a rules engine; no in-world text diagrams the color pie.

**One deliberate carve-out (authorial, ~85% agreement):** threads *in art* do carry the five colors on purpose — that's ludonarrative integration, and it's wanted. See [[art-language]] §5. The carve-out is visual, not verbal: the art may show a crimson thread; no villager calls it "a red mana source."

## For card-makers

- Show magic as **event and craft**, never as system. A spell looks like something *happening*, and casting looks like *work* — gesture, rite, tool, cost — not like menu selection.
- An ordinary person's week contains magic the way a medieval week contained the saints: ambient, real, unexplained, woven into chores and oaths and weather.
- Wonder and fear are both available, because nothing is domesticated by explanation. The card that treats magic as routine (Mortician's Assistant's long shift) and the card that treats it as terrifying (Soul Reaper) are both right.
- Characters may hold *theories* — that's testimony ([[flavor-text]]), and theories may conflict. What's forbidden is the world containing a *correct, confirmed* theory.
