# The Fall — the shape of the past *(working label; the event itself stays unnamed)*

## A time of disarray *(authorial)*

> "The world is in a bad spot right now. It's a time of disarray — not between 'Good and Evil' or 'White and Blue against Red and Black', but in the broader sense of 'man against man'." — Joe

*Observed:* the corpus complies completely — there is no Dark Lord and no evil faction in 290 cards. Final Strike's executioner and victim are interchangeable soldiers; the [[white]]/[[black]] bypass duality is administrative, not metaphysical. This also protects [[the-divine]]: a war of Good vs Evil would *prove* something about the gods; man against man proves nothing, which is the point.

And "disarray" implies a former *array* — which is the next section. Much of the violence is men fighting over who inherits the wreckage.

## Something fell *(authorial — what, exactly, deliberately unknown even to the creators)*

> "There was probably the fall of *something*. An empire, a religion — I don't think we know what yet. There is a past full of grand marble buildings. The present takes place in royal halls — grand with tapestry, to be sure, but that tapestry hangs over rough-hewn stone. Emperors have fallen to kings, kings will fall to warlords, and nature bides its time, waiting for its turn after the warlords." — Joe

*Observed, the marble past:* **Oblation** is the key exhibit — a man making offerings at the *weathered, oxidized, verdigris-crusted* base of a colossal bronze statue. Bronze takes generations to green; the monument has outlived its own meaning, and the worship continues anyway ([[the-divine]]). Also: City of Brass's bleached classical tiers; Martyred Scholar's grand marble stairs; Phylactery's cracked, moss-slicked altar already half-claimed by swamp; Iron Sentinel's enormous wall patrolled by something older than its garrison.

Discipline: **what fell stays unnamed** — empire, church, both, the same thing — and in-world accounts should *conflict* (see §narrator below). Don't date it. Don't map it.

## The Fall is a direction, not an event *(proposal)*

Emperors → kings → warlords is a sequence *still running*. The grand fall (marble, colossi) is old; the library's sack ([[blue]]) is recent — the same pattern on a smaller rung. Each generation watches something smaller fall the way something larger fell before. The disarray isn't aftermath; it's the pattern continuing downward. And [[green]] waits at the end of the line, in no hurry — nature's turn comes after the warlords.

## Meridian at three in the afternoon *(authorial)*

> "If the set mainly takes place at metaphorical/narrative/historical dusk, Meridian is, perhaps, at 3:00 PM. It is not dark, not even close. And yet, it has passed noon." — Joe

Reading this back onto the existing card: the **Equatorial Engine** bottles noon — [[meridian]] *manufactures the hour it refuses to admit losing*. The shadows lengthen anyway. (Note the world-clock is **metaphorical/narrative/historical** — not literal lighting; that boundary lives in [[art-language]] §1. It also half-revives a buried name; see [[names-ledger]] on "the Long Dusk.")

A small tragic rhyme worth keeping: [[white]]'s art lives at *dawn* — rallying speeches, first light, frost burning off — in a world whose hour is dusk. White's aesthetic is a memory of morning that the history contradicts.

## A world without a narrator *(proposal — the unifying epistemology)*

The same posture now repeats in every register:

- **Threads** — everyone has seen one once; nobody believes it ([[threads]]).
- **Gods** — testimony everywhere, verdict nowhere ([[the-divine]]).
- **History** — ruins everywhere, no agreed account of what fell (this page).

One sentence for all three: **this is a world that has lost its narrator.** No surviving authority can say what the threads are, what the gods intend, or what the marble was for. The set's voice should honor that — evidence everywhere, verdict nowhere — which is also the argument behind the [[flavor-text]] opinion.
