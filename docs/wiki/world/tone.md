# Tone — small windows on a large war

## The small-windows discipline *(adopted — the bible's first law)*

A card is a very small window. Big things may happen in this world — but the card never depicts the big thing; it depicts a *moment* inside it.

> "It's hard to do a card that does 'The American Revolution'. But I can probably make a card that says 'throw tea off a boat'." — Joe

So: this bible is **a book of rooms, not maps**. Its job is to make the next hundred small scenes more coherent — not to make the world bigger. World bibles drift toward continents, ancient empires, and capital-P Prophecies; resist all of it, the same way the art skill resists adjective-stacking. A page earns its place here by improving a *card*.

The corpus already obeys this. Its best pieces are one candle on a morgue slab (Mortician's Assistant), one goblin sad at a bar (Burnout Shaman), one judge with a bloody sword in a village square (Righteous Judge), one puppy under a stray bolt (Anger Hound).

## The war *(authorial)*

This is a time of war and conflict, magical and mundane — and of **disarray**:

> "Not between 'Good and Evil' or 'White and Blue against Red and Black', but in the broader sense of 'man against man'." — Joe

> "Gods and kings may decide the fates of heaven and earth. Their wars are fought by men, who die face-down in the mud. There is no tension there." — Joe

There is no Dark Lord in this world and no evil faction; the disarray has a *shape* and a past — see [[the-fall]].

*Observed:* the war's everyman is **"a soldier in dented steel plate armor"** — that exact phrase recurs across the prompt corpus like a ballad refrain (Doom Blade's victim, Dread Wraith's prey, Dawn Angel's patient, Final Strike's kneeler, Carrion Feeder's meal, Otherworldly Journey's sleeper). The armor is always *dented*, never gleaming. Keep him. He is everyone.

*Open:* whether the war is one named thing or the world's permanent weather. Lean: small windows never need the war's name — what a card shows is mud, frost, a bell, a breach. (See [[names-ledger]] on naming restraint generally.)

## Magic is a verb *(authorial, still settling)*

When things happen in this world, magic happens. Magic isn't a separate register reached for on special occasions; it's how events express themselves (Way of Kings is a partial touchstone). **But** the setting deliberately holds both very low-fantasy material (mud, taxes, dented steel, pots smashed in a kitchen) and very high-fantasy material (gods, immortals, men rewoven into brass). How that tension is shown — what an ordinary person sees in an ordinary week — is an open question, maybe *the* open question.

## Interiority — even the monsters *(observed; protect this)*

The corpus consistently grants inner lives to things other sets treat as set-dressing: a goblin having "a full mental breakdown" (Raging Goblin), a sleep-paralysis demon at work (Soul Reaper, straight out of Fuseli), a vampire absorbed in his painting (Blood Artist), a zombie at the end of a long shift (Mortician's Assistant). Gallows humor is permitted and load-bearing (Goblin Piercer "is not very good at it"). The set is *humane* — its monsters have interiority and its heroes are tired. That range — Fuseli to sad-goblin-at-the-bar — is the tone.

## Touchstones *(authorial unless noted)*

- **The Banner Saga** — the threads imagery ([[threads]]).
- **The Way of Kings** — magic-as-event, partially.
- **Hitchhiker's Guide's hyperspace bypass** — the [[black]]/[[white]] duality: "I simply do not care that you live there" vs. "unfortunately, it has to go somewhere."
- **The Carryx** (*The Mercy of Gods*) — black's honest callousness: what is, is.
- **"The Snow it Melts the Soonest"** — green's tonal register ([[green]]).
- *(Observed)* **Fuseli's "The Nightmare"** — Soul Reaper quotes it; the set's horror is art-historical, intimate, candle-lit.
