# The divine — a theology of absence *(authorial; deliberately unresolved)*

The corpus's clearest theological statement is **Anger of the Gods**: a serene deity on a cloud bank, arms out in a calm gesture, while a city burns beneath her — *"unimportant to the deific figure above."* The gods do not intervene. The angels do: every angel in the set is down in the war doing work (Dawn Angel and Benevolent Angel healing the wounded, Binding Angel making an arrest).

## Why don't the gods act? *(authorial — keep all three doors open)*

> "Perhaps this is all according to some plan. Perhaps they don't care. Perhaps, if angels can break things when they are careless, one might wonder what would happen if the divine itself touched the world." — Joe

Three readings, none confirmed, all live:

1. **The plan.** It's all according to something. (No one can check.)
2. **Indifference.** Anger of the Gods, read literally.
3. **Restraint as mercy.** Angels are powerful beings — radiant, like Binding Angel atop her quarry — but *the divine is a blunt instrument, and man is a fragile thing*. If careless angels break what they handle, the gods' absence might be the gentlest thing about them.

## The authorial note underneath *(authorial — this is the emotional register)*

> "The note is of someone who *wants* to be religious, but… why doesn't any of it seem to *work*? Like why doesn't it… *do* anything?" — Joe

This is the world's religious experience: not atheism, not faith — **unanswered reaching**. Prayers are sincere. Shrines are tended (Ancestral Priest's quiet bloom of light is real). And heaven is silent on the question of whether any of it matters. White's institutions ([[white]]) are built in that silence and have to act anyway.

## Discipline — depiction is allowed; adjudication is the forbidden move

**The unresolvedness is the point — don't resolve it.** But the line needs drawing carefully, because Anger of the Gods *depicts a god* and is one of the set's best pieces. The first draft of this rule ("explaining the gods is erosion") aimed at the right thing from the wrong line. Refined (2026-06-09, after Joe pressed on the contradiction):

- **Depiction is allowed.** Gods may appear, act, loom. Anger of the Gods works because the image is compatible with all three doors above — the serenity could be the plan, could be indifference, could be restraint. The art *deepens* the question.
- **Watch what that card's name does:** the deity shows no anger at all. "Anger of the Gods" is the *mortals'* name for the event — survivors reading a burning city as wrath. The gap between the title and the image is the entire theology in one small window. That gap is the house move.
- **Interpretation must stay diegetic.** Cards may carry confident claims — a zealot's certainty, a priest's doctrine, a heretic's sneer — because those are *testimony* from inside the world. Per [[tone]], every card is one witness.
- **Adjudication is the forbidden move:** any card whose art + name + rules + flavor *jointly* settle which door is true — or any pair of cards that agree so completely the question closes. Individual witnesses may be certain; the corpus stays a hung jury.

**Cards may testify; the set never returns a verdict.** Same standing order as the Heir's notes: don't soften it — and don't settle it.
